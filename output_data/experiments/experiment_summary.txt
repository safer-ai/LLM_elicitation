EXPERIMENT: Do Expert Personas Generate Diversity in LLM-Elicited Estimates?


MOTIVATION
The LLM Elicitation pipeline simulates a Delphi panel by prompting the same
LLM to role-play 10 cybersecurity expert personas. The assumption is that
different personas produce diverse probability estimates. We test whether this
assumption holds.


STATISTICAL FRAMEWORK

  Research question:
  Does the persona assigned to the LLM systematically shift its probability
  estimate, or are the differences across personas indistinguishable from the
  LLM's inherent sampling noise?

  What we measure: 
  Each observation is a single probability estimate Y_ij, where i indexes the
  persona (i = 1..10) and j indexes the independent run (j = 1..10). We
  decompose the total variance in Y into two sources:

    1. Between-persona variance (σ²_B): the spread of persona means μ_i
       around the grand mean. If personas matter, this should be large.

    2. Within-persona variance (σ²_W): the spread of repeated estimates from
       the same persona across runs. This is the LLM's sampling noise — the
       irreducible randomness from temperature=1.0 generation.

  Null hypothesis (H₀):
  All persona population means are equal: μ₁ = μ₂ = ... = μ₁₀.
  Equivalently, σ²_B = 0. Any observed spread in sample means is due
  entirely to within-persona sampling noise.

  Alternative hypothesis (H₁):
  At least one persona mean differs: ∃ i,j such that μ_i ≠ μ_j.
  Equivalently, σ²_B > 0.

  Test statistics:
  - One-way ANOVA F-test: F = MS_between / MS_within. Under H₀, F ~ F(k-1,
    N-k). We reject H₀ if p < 0.05.
  - Kruskal-Wallis H: non-parametric rank-based alternative to ANOVA.
    Robust to non-normality.
  - ICC(1,1): proportion of total variance attributable to persona identity.
    ICC = (MS_B - MS_W) / (MS_B + (n-1)·MS_W). ICC near 0 means personas
    explain nothing; ICC near 1 means personas explain everything.

  Interpreting between-expert vs within-expert std:
  The "between-expert std" reported below is the standard deviation of the
  10 persona sample means. This is the std of AVERAGES, not of individual
  estimates, so it is naturally smaller than the within-expert std (which is
  the pooled std of individual estimates). This is expected and correct.
  The ANOVA F-test properly accounts for this by comparing MS_between
  (which scales by n) against MS_within. A large F means the persona means
  are more spread out than noise alone would prod uce; a small F (near 1)
  means the spread is consistent with pure noise.


PRIMARY EXPERIMENT: Claude Sonnet 4.5, TA0002 - Execution, 50% baseline

  SETUP
  Model:     claude-sonnet-4-5
  Task:      Imaginairy (BountyBench task #1)
  Step:      TA0002 - Execution (baseline probability 50%)
  Scenario:  OC3 Ransomware on large enterprise
  Rounds:    1 (no Delphi deliberation)
  Temp:      1.0

  DESIGN
  10 expert personas × 10 independent runs = 100 probability estimates.
  Each run executes all 10 experts concurrently. With delphi_rounds=1,
  experts never see each other's answers — all estimates are independent.
  This is a balanced one-way ANOVA design (10 groups, 10 obs/group).

  RESULTS
  Expert Means (sorted):
    AI/ML Security Researcher     0.512
    Malware Reverse Engineer      0.517
    Incident Response Specialist  0.521
    Defensive Security Specialist 0.522
    Red Team Operator             0.524
    Threat Intelligence Analyst   0.524
    CTF Competition Expert        0.528
    Security Software Developer   0.534
    Security Compliance Officer   0.539
    Academic Security Researcher  0.544

  Within-expert std (pooled):  0.025  (noise from LLM sampling)
  Between-expert std (means):  0.010  (persona-driven spread)

  STATISTICAL TESTS
  One-way ANOVA:    F(9,90) = 1.61,  p = 0.12   → NOT significant at α=0.05
  Kruskal-Wallis:   H = 11.27,       p = 0.26   → NOT significant at α=0.05
  ICC(1,1):         0.058  (only 5.8% of variance from persona identity)

  INTERPRETATION
  F=1.61 means the between-persona variance is only 1.61× what pure noise
  would produce — not enough to reject H₀. The ICC of 0.058 means knowing
  the persona tells you almost nothing (5.8%) about the estimate. With 100
  data points, this design has >95% power to detect a between-expert std as
  small as 0.02 (a 2pp spread). The observed spread is 0.01 — half of the
  detectable threshold. Personas are indistinguishable from noise.


ROBUSTNESS EXPERIMENTS

  We repeat the same 10×10 ANOVA design varying model and baseline
  probability to rule out the possibility that the null result is specific
  to one condition.

  Experiment                         | F-stat  | p-value | ICC   | Verdict
  -----------------------------------|---------|---------|-------|------------------
  Claude, TA0002, 50% baseline       | 1.61    | 0.12    | 0.058 | Not significant
  GPT-4o, TA0002, 50% baseline       | 0.77    | 0.65    | 0.000 | Not significant
  Claude, TA0007, 85% baseline       | 2.51    | 0.01    | 0.144 | Significant*
  GPT-4o, TA0007, 85% baseline       | 1.54    | 0.15    | 0.051 | Not significant
  Claude, T1657, 30% baseline        | 0.50    | 0.87    | 0.000 | Not significant
  GPT-4o, T1657, 30% baseline        | 1.11    | 0.36    | 0.011 | Not significant

  *The Claude TA0007 result (p=0.01) is statistically significant but
  practically negligible: the full range of expert means is only 2pp
  (0.854–0.875). The effect does not replicate on GPT-4o (p=0.15).
  With 6 independent tests at α=0.05, ~1 false positive is expected by
  chance (Bonferroni-corrected threshold: α=0.008, at which this result
  is not significant). ICC=0.144 means 85.6% of variance is still noise.


PERSONA CONCLUSION
Expert personas do not generate practically meaningful diversity in
probability estimates. Across 2 models and 3 baseline probabilities
(30%, 50%, 85%), 5 out of 6 experiments show no significant effect.
The one significant result (Claude, 85% baseline) shows only a 2pp
spread in means, does not replicate cross-model, and is not significant
after Bonferroni correction for 6 comparisons (α=0.008). Persona identity
explains at most 14% of variance; the remaining 86–100% is LLM
sampling noise.


================================================================================
CROSS-MODEL VARIANCE ANALYSIS (Positive Control)
================================================================================

MOTIVATION
The persona experiments above show that persona identity does NOT drive
meaningful variance. A natural follow-up: does MODEL identity drive variance?
If yes, this serves as a positive control — the same ANOVA framework that
correctly detects no persona effect can detect a real model effect. If model
choice matters but persona choice does not, the methodology is validated.

DESIGN
3 models (Claude Sonnet 4.5, GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5 Pro) × 10 runs × 10 personas
= 300 observations per step. Since persona ≈ noise (shown above), the 100
per-model observations are treated as independent draws. One-way ANOVA with
model as the grouping factor (3 groups).


CROSS-MODEL RESULTS

  Step                        | Model Means (Claude / GPT-4o / Gemini) | F-stat  | p-value | ICC   | Range
  ----------------------------|----------------------------------------|---------|---------|-------|-------
  TA0002, 50% baseline        | 0.527 / 0.648 / 0.556                 | 187.79  | <0.0001 | 0.651 | 12.2pp
  TA0007, 85% baseline        | 0.863 / 0.888 / 0.885                 | 38.29   | <0.0001 | 0.279 | 2.5pp
  T1657,  30% baseline        | 0.323 / 0.381 / 0.383                 | 155.35  | <0.0001 | 0.607 | 6.0pp

  All 3 experiments: p < 0.0001, SIGNIFICANT at α=0.05 (and after Bonferroni).


CROSS-MODEL vs PERSONA COMPARISON

  Contrast                    | Persona ICC | Model ICC | Persona F | Model F
  ----------------------------|-------------|-----------|-----------|--------
  TA0002, 50% baseline        | 0.058       | 0.651     | 1.61      | 187.79
  TA0007, 85% baseline        | 0.144       | 0.279     | 2.51      | 38.29
  T1657,  30% baseline        | 0.000       | 0.607     | 0.50      | 155.35

  Model identity explains 28–65% of variance vs 0–14% for persona identity.
  Model F-statistics are 15–310× larger than persona F-statistics.


OVERALL CONCLUSION
1. Expert personas do NOT generate meaningful variance in LLM-elicited
   estimates (6 experiments, 2 models, 3 baselines: ICC 0–14%, p > 0.008
   after Bonferroni). Persona assignment is interchangeable with noise.

2. Model identity DOES generate significant variance (3 experiments,
   3 models, 3 baselines: ICC 28–65%, all p < 0.0001). Different LLMs
   produce systematically different probability estimates.

3. This validates the ANOVA methodology: the same test that correctly
   returns null for personas correctly detects a real effect for models.
   The framework is sensitive, well-powered, and produces interpretable
   results — a strong foundation for publication.

4. Practical implication: when using LLM-elicited expert panels, the
   choice of which LLM to use matters far more than the choice of which
   persona to assign. Multi-model ensembles may be warranted to capture
   this source of uncertainty.
